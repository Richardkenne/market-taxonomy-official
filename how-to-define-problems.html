How We Define Monetizable Problems

This page explains how we decide which problems are worth building businesses around.

Not ideas. Not feature requests. Not generic "needs". Only problems that show real economic signals.

Why This Exists

A market taxonomy is useful only if it leads to actionable opportunities. Without a clear definition of what a real, monetizable problem is, you get:

  • Idea lists
  • Feature wishlists
  • Vague market descriptions

None of which reliably produce products people actually pay for. This framework exists to prevent that.

The Core Definition

A monetizable problem is a recurring struggle that already changes behavior and carries an economic cost for a clearly identifiable budget owner.

If behavior doesn't change, the problem is theoretical. If there's no cost, monetization is weak.

The 5 Indicators We Look For

These indicators are ordered by signal strength, not popularity.

1. Observable Behavior Change

People are already doing something to deal with the problem:

  • Spreadsheets
  • WhatsApp / email workarounds
  • Manual processes
  • Wrong or repurposed tools
  • Extra people hired just to "handle it"

Why this matters: Behavior changes before money changes hands. No workaround usually means no urgency.

2. Economic Cost

At least one of these is true:

  • Time is being wasted
  • Money is being lost
  • Risk is increasing (errors, downtime, compliance issues, churn)

Why this matters: If the problem has no cost, there is no reason to allocate budget to fix it.

3. Recurrence

The problem happens daily, weekly, or monthly. Not once a year. Not edge cases.

Why this matters: Recurrence is what makes problems SaaS-friendly and scalable.

4. Clear Budget Owner

It's clear who feels the pain, who decides, and who pays. They can be different people, but they must be identifiable.

Why this matters: Unclear payers = long sales cycles and weak monetization.

5. Persistent Friction

The problem creates ongoing operational stress: frustration, anxiety, constant firefighting.

Why this matters: Problems that annoy people every day convert better than rare disasters.

How We Validate Problems

We use a simple rule:

A problem is activated only if at least 4 out of 5 indicators are present.

0–3 indicators → archived (non-priority)
4–5 indicators → active (worth building around)

This keeps the taxonomy clean and prevents inflation.

What We Don't Count as Problems

We explicitly exclude:

  • Feature requests without measurable loss
  • Generic desires ("better UX", "more growth")
  • One-off events
  • High-level market statements with no operator context

If it can't be tied to a recurring, costly struggle, it doesn't enter the system.

The One-Sentence Test

Every valid problem must be expressible as: "This operator loses X (time / money / risk) because Y, in context Z, on a recurring basis."

If you can't write this sentence clearly, the problem isn't defined yet.

Where This Framework Comes From

This approach is a synthesis of widely adopted principles used by founders, investors, and operators.

Foundations: How Real Problems Form

  • Clayton Christensen — Jobs to Be Done
  • Bob Moesta — Demand discovery and struggle moments
  • Rob Fitzpatrick — The Mom Test

Startup & Product Validation

  • Y Combinator — Problem–solution fit, desperate users, early willingness to pay
  • Eric Ries — The Lean Startup
  • IDEO — Observed behavior over stated preferences

Venture & Market Structure

  • Sequoia Capital — Market pain and buyer clarity
  • Andreessen Horowitz — Market structure and budget ownership
  • Bessemer Venture Partners — SaaS laws: recurrence and expansion

Enterprise & Real Operations

  • McKinsey & Company — Operational inefficiencies and cost-to-serve
  • Gartner — Enterprise buying behavior and category formation
  • Harvard Business Review — Theory validated by real cases

All of these sources converge on the same signals, even if they use different language.

Final Rule

A problem is real only if it already changes behavior.

A problem is monetizable only if that behavior has a cost.

This rule governs which problems enter the taxonomy—and which are ignored.
