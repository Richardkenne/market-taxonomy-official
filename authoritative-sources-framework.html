How Market Categories Are Recognized

Authoritative Sources Framework

Section 1

Purpose of This Document

This document defines the authoritative source framework used by the Market Taxonomy system to recognize, validate, and structure economic markets.

Its purpose is not to predict trends, promote narratives, or rank opportunities. Its purpose is to ensure that every market category represented in the system is:

  • Externally recognizable
  • Institutionally and economically grounded
  • Non-arbitrary
  • Consistent across time

The framework governs how sources are selected, how they are interpreted, and how conflicting perspectives are resolved.

Section 2

What "Authority" Means in Market Definition

A market category is considered authoritative when it is recognized by multiple independent classes of actors who participate in, regulate, measure, or allocate capital within that market.

No single actor defines a market alone.
Authority emerges from convergence, not declaration.

Section 3

The Four Classes of Authoritative Sources

The system recognizes four distinct classes of sources, each with a specific role in market definition.

Institutional & Classification Authorities
Global classification systems, statistical agencies, intergovernmental bodies, sector regulators.
Role
Define boundaries · Stabilize naming · Enable cross-country comparison
Limit
Slow to adapt · Often lag emerging sub-markets
Market Intelligence & Research Firms
Sector-specific research firms, analyst organizations, benchmarking and forecasting providers.
Role
Identify segments · Measure scale and growth · Track demand patterns
Limit
Less sensitive to early structural shifts
Operators & Industry Participants
Leading companies, industry associations, trade bodies, infrastructure operators.
Role
Validate operational relevance · Reveal workflow structure · Confirm economic substance
Limit
Partial by nature · Biased toward their position in the value chain
Venture Capital & Investment Actors
Venture capital firms, growth equity investors, investment theses and portfolios.
Role
Signal investability · Anticipate market emergence · Accelerate semantic convergence
Limit
Narrative-driven · Not suitable as sole definers of categories

Section 4

The Principle of Convergent Validation

A market category is considered valid in the taxonomy when it is recognized by at least two different source classes.

  • Institutional + Market Intelligence
  • Operators + Venture Capital
  • Market Intelligence + Operators

No category is defined using only one source class. This rule prevents VC-only narratives, operator self-labeling, and purely theoretical classifications.

Section 5

The Role of Venture Capital (Clarified)

Venture capital sources are explicitly included in the framework. However, their role is not to define markets unilaterally, but to confirm that capital treats a segment as investable, signal early institutionalization, and reveal future structural importance.

VC sources are treated as legitimizers, not primary classifiers.
Markets do not exist because VCs name them. Markets exist when capital, operators, and structure intersect.

Section 6

Macro Markets vs Overlays

The taxonomy distinguishes between two types of categories:

Type Definition Examples
Macro Markets Foundational economic domains recognized across institutions, operators, and capital. Form the Level 0 structure. Real Estate · Energy · Healthcare · Financial Services · Software
Overlays (Lenses) Cross-cutting themes that apply across multiple macro markets. Analytical lenses, not structural domains. FinTech · Cybersecurity · AI · Climate Technology

Overlays do not replace macro markets and are not treated as standalone Level 0 categories.

Section 7

Source Selection Rule (Operational)

For each Macro Market, the system aims to reference sources that collectively cover: structural definition, market measurement, operational reality, and capital recognition.

There is no fixed quota, but the system avoids mono-source representation. Source relevance is preferred over source symmetry.

Section 8

How Sources Are Used Across Levels

Level Purpose Role of Sources
Level 0 Market existence External recognition
Level 1 Category structure Segmentation logic
Level 2 Sub-market clarity Institutional validation
Level 3 Segment grounding Contextual verification

Sources do not generate problems, products, or solutions. They validate structure, not ideas.

Section 9

Governance & Evolution

Markets evolve. The taxonomy must adapt without breaking consistency.

  • New categories require multi-source validation
  • Naming changes must reflect external convergence
  • Overlays may emerge without altering macro structure
  • No category is added based on narrative alone

The system favors stability with controlled adaptation.

Note

Note on Level 1 Category Count

The number of Level 1 categories is not predefined. Across most macro markets, convergent validation by institutional authorities, market intelligence, operators, and capital allocation actors typically results in a limited set of stable categories. This recurring pattern reflects structural clarity and institutional practice, not an imposed numerical constraint.

Markets are not invented.
They are recognized.

Recognition emerges when institutions, operators, and capital independently agree that a domain exists. This document defines how that recognition is enforced within the Market Taxonomy system.
