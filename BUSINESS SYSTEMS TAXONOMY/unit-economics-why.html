Unit Economics

Why These Numbers?

The benchmarks inside each Business System card are not targets. They are structural viability constraints.

The core argument

These benchmarks are not arbitrary targets and they are not industry-specific.

They emerge consistently wherever a Business System is used, across different markets, geographies, and technologies. Unit economics are system-driven: once the monetisation mechanism is fixed, the same economic constraints appear again and again.

This is why similar thresholds — margins, payback, retention, yield, take rate — recur in otherwise unrelated businesses. A SaaS company in healthcare and a SaaS company in logistics face the same NRR pressure. A marketplace in fashion and a marketplace in freight face the same cold-start and take-rate constraints.

What the numbers represent

The numbers shown in each card reflect structural viability, not optimisation. Falling materially outside these ranges usually indicates that the system is either:

  • mispriced,
  • absorbing hidden costs,
  • or compensating for a deeper structural weakness.

These benchmarks are therefore best read as viability constraints, not growth goals. A business that does not meet them is not necessarily failing — but it must explain why its structure is an exception, not assume it is.

Why these sources (and not others)

The ranges used here are supported by convergent evidence from a small set of top-tier, independent authorities spanning strategy, venture capital, economics, and accounting.

Sources were intentionally limited to those that define how businesses actually work, not commentary or industry blogs. Beyond ~7 top-tier independent sources, additional citations repeat the same structural conclusions.

  • 1. Harvard Business School Case-based analysis of business systems and their economic constraints across industries and geographies.
  • 2. McKinsey & Company Empirical benchmarks on margins, payback, and sustainability by monetisation model — applied across hundreds of corporate engagements.
  • 3. Bessemer Venture Partners Long-running benchmark frameworks (State of the Cloud, recurring revenue scorecards) — the most cited SaaS and subscription economics reference in venture.
  • 4. Sequoia Capital System-level analysis of what makes businesses durable at scale — used in investment committee frameworks globally.
  • 5. Andreessen Horowitz (a16z) Structural breakdown of monetisation systems and their economic limits — published across software, marketplace, and fintech verticals.
  • 6. IFRS / US GAAP Formal definitions of revenue, margin, and cost recognition across business types. The legal and accounting floor for what counts as a valid unit.
  • 7. OECD Cross-sector economic analysis of value creation and sustainability — used by governments, regulators, and institutional investors.

Final rule (non-negotiable)

Unit economics are system-specific.

If the core metrics don't work, the Business System doesn't work — regardless of TAM, team, or technology.
