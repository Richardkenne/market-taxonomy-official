Franchise Dealerships — Niches

OEM-authorized new vehicle retailers ranging from single-point family operators to publicly traded mega dealer groups. Facing disruption from direct-to-consumer EV brands, digital retailing, and agency model experiments.

These sources define how this niche is structured, sized, and tracked by industry analysts and trade associations.

Sources

NADA Data Cox Automotive J.D. Power S&P Global Mobility

Market Context

Size / Scale$1.2T+ US new vehicle retail; ~16,700 franchise dealers
Growth signalDealer count declining 1-2% YoY; per-dealer revenue rising with consolidation
Key playersAutoNation, Lithia, Penske, Group 1, Hendrick, Sonic
GeographyUS-centric franchise model; EU shifting to agency model
Franchise dealerships are caught between OEMs experimenting with direct sales (Tesla, Rivian) and agency models (Mercedes EU), while service and F&I departments generate the majority of dealer profits.

Known Pain Points

  • Digital retailing integration with legacy DMS (CDK, Reynolds) creating friction in online-to-showroom handoff
  • EV service readiness — technician training, high-voltage certification, and charging infrastructure at dealerships
  • Inventory management in a build-to-order transition reducing days supply but requiring deposit management
  • Customer data ownership battles between OEMs pushing connected services and dealers owning the relationship
  • Fixed operations efficiency — service lane throughput, parts inventory, and technician productivity

How to Choose a Niche

Service department workflow automation and digital retailing tools for mid-size dealer groups (4-20 rooftops). These dealers are large enough to invest in technology but too small to build custom solutions, creating a large addressable market for SaaS tools.
