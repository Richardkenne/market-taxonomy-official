Wheat & Small Grains

Global wheat production exceeds 780 million metric tons annually, making it the second most-produced cereal. Small grains (barley, oats, rye, sorghum) add another 300M+ tons, serving flour milling, malting, animal feed, and emerging plant-based food markets.

Data drawn from the International Grains Council, USDA Foreign Agricultural Service wheat outlook, and FAO cereal production indices.

Wheat quality classification (protein content, falling number, test weight) directly determines price premiums of $0.50-$2.00/bushel, yet most growers lack real-time quality testing tools at harvest. This information gap costs the average wheat farm $5,000-$15,000/year in missed premiums.

Known Pain Points

  • Protein content variability within fields makes it hard to segregate grain for premium markets
  • Disease pressure (fusarium, rust, septoria) requires precise fungicide timing that most growers guess
  • Export market access depends on phytosanitary compliance that small producers struggle to navigate
  • Climate-driven yield instability increasing 15-20% per decade across major wheat belts

How to Choose a Niche

Wheat growers are price-takers in a commodity market, so the highest-value niches involve quality differentiation (protein testing, identity-preserved programs) or cost reduction (variable-rate seeding, precision fungicide application). The craft malting and artisan flour movements are creating small but growing premium channels.
