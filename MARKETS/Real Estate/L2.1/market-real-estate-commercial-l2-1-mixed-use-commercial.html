Part A — Level 2.1
Mixed-Use Commercial

Mixed-use commercial developments combine two or more property types — typically retail, office, residential, and hotel — in a single project or district, creating live-work-play environments in urban and suburban settings.

Market sizing draws on ULI mixed-use development surveys, ICSC retail-residential integration research, and CoStar mixed-use project tracking across major US metros.

Sources

ULI Mixed-Use Research ICSC Research CoStar Mixed-Use Analytics Cushman & Wakefield Mixed-Use

Market Context

Size / Scale Mixed-use accounts for ~15–20% of new commercial development; individual projects range from $50M to $5B+; major urban mixed-use districts represent $10B+ in asset value
Growth signal Suburban densification and transit-oriented development driving new mixed-use supply; adaptive reuse of retail malls into mixed-use growing as redevelopment strategy
Key players Related Companies (developer, Hudson Yards), Brookfield Properties (owner/operator), Forest City (developer), Hines (developer/manager), Tanger (retail-to-mixed-use conversion)
Geography Urban infill in gateway cities (NYC, Chicago, DC, Miami); suburban town center redevelopments; transit-oriented developments along light rail and commuter rail corridors
Mixed-use properties require managing fundamentally different tenant types (retail, residential, office, hotel) under one ownership structure — creating asset management, accounting, and operations complexity that no single platform handles well.

Known Pain Points

  • Accounting and financial reporting for mixed-use assets requires multiple chart-of-accounts structures within a single entity — most property accounting software forces operators to choose one asset class
  • Shared-area maintenance cost allocation (CAM charges, utilities, security) across different tenant types with different lease structures requires complex calculations done manually
  • Parking management for a mixed-use development serving retail shoppers, office tenants, hotel guests, and residents simultaneously requires dynamic allocation with no adequate software solution
  • Leasing and tenant coordination across retail, office, and residential teams within the same development often involves three separate brokerages with no unified pipeline or availability data

How to Choose a Niche

Target mixed-use developers and operators directly — they are frustrated buyers who know their problem is unsolved. Asset management software that handles multiple property types in one ledger is the core opportunity. Alternatively, build a parking or common area revenue management tool specifically for mixed-use properties — it is discrete, measurable, and has clear ROI.
