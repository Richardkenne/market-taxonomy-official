Part A — Level 2.1
Last-Mile Distribution

Last-mile distribution facilities are small-format urban infill industrial properties — typically 20,000–150,000 sq ft — located within dense population centers to enable same-day and next-day delivery to consumers.

Market data is drawn from CBRE Urban Logistics reports, JLL Last-Mile research, and Prologis urban logistics demand studies tracking proximity-to-consumer delivery trends.

Sources

CBRE Urban Logistics Prologis Logistics & Last-Mile Research JLL Urban Industrial Outlook NAIOP Industrial Research

Market Context

Size / Scale Urban infill industrial represents ~10–15% of total industrial inventory but commands 20–40% rent premiums; ~500M sq ft of last-mile relevant stock in top 25 US metros
Growth signal Fastest-growing industrial subtype; same-day delivery expectations from Amazon and Instacart pulling demand forward; brownfield and adaptive reuse supplying most new stock
Key players Amazon (occupier, ~400M sq ft globally), Prologis (owner, Urban Logistics Fund), Blackstone (private owner, BioMed/last-mile focus), Dermody Properties (developer), Ares Management (owner)
Geography NYC boroughs (Bronx, Brooklyn), LA basin (Vernon, Compton, Carson), Chicago, Dallas, Miami, SF Bay Area; proximity to dense residential population is the defining site criterion
Last-mile facilities are the most constrained and highest-value industrial real estate, where landlords and tenants need site selection, utilization, and lease management tools calibrated for urban infill complexity.

Known Pain Points

  • Site selection for urban last-mile facilities requires simultaneous analysis of zoning, proximity-to-consumer data, truck access routing, and land availability data that exists in siloed public and private databases
  • Adaptive reuse feasibility analysis for converting urban commercial or retail buildings to last-mile use (structural, mechanical, dock access) has no purpose-built workflow — deals are slow and expensive to underwrite
  • Multi-story urban warehouse leasing and tenant coordination (vertical stacking of different tenants with shared ramp and dock access) has no adequate operating or legal framework in existing tools
  • Last-mile tenant operational intensity (high truck traffic, extended hours, neighborhood impact) creates landlord-tenant friction and community relations issues with no management platform

How to Choose a Niche

Site selection analytics combining consumer proximity, zoning, and traffic/access data is the most differentiated opportunity — existing tools (CoStar, LoopNet) are not built for last-mile optimization criteria. Alternatively, target the adaptive reuse feasibility workflow, which is done entirely by consultants with no software support and represents tens of millions of dollars in consulting fees annually.
