Strip Malls

Strip malls are open-air, single-row retail centers typically 5,000–100,000 sq ft, anchored by convenience-oriented tenants such as nail salons, dry cleaners, pizza shops, and small-format service retailers.

Market data is sourced from ICSC neighborhood and strip center research, CoStar retail availability analytics, and Marcus & Millichap strip center investment reports.

Sources

ICSC Neighborhood Center Research Marcus & Millichap Retail Research CoStar Retail Analytics Calkain Commercial Research

Market Context

Size / Scale ~75,000–100,000 strip mall properties in the US; total GLA ~5–7B sq ft; highly fragmented ownership with millions of small private landlords
Growth signal Resilient demand from service and convenience tenants immune to e-commerce; vacancy at multi-year lows (~5%); cap rates compressing as investors rotate from office
Key players Whitestone REIT (community centers), Inland Real Estate (private, large strip portfolio), numerous regional private owners; most ownership is highly fragmented among small investors
Geography Ubiquitous across US suburbs; highest density in Southeast, Midwest, and Texas; suburban population growth markets seeing new strip development
Strip mall ownership is highly fragmented — millions of properties owned by small private investors who manage leasing, maintenance, and tenant relations with almost no technology.

Known Pain Points

  • Small private strip mall owners manage leases, CAM reconciliations, and tenant communications with spreadsheets and email — no adequate software exists for the 1–20 property owner segment
  • CAM reconciliation for strip malls with 5–15 tenants on NNN or modified gross leases involves manual expense allocation and annual true-up calculations prone to billing errors and tenant disputes
  • Tenant mix management and vacancy backfill is entirely relationship-driven with no data tools — owners rely on local brokers and personal networks with no market demand or comp data integration
  • Deferred maintenance tracking and CapEx planning for aging strip mall roof systems, parking lots, and facade improvements is informal, leading to reactive emergency spending rather than planned capital allocation

How to Choose a Niche

The fragmented small-landlord strip mall market is a massive, underserved opportunity — but also hard to reach and monetize at low ARPU. Consider a freemium model targeting owners of 1–5 strip malls and monetizing via transaction services (insurance, financing referrals, contractor marketplace). Alternatively, target strip mall REITs and regional owners of 20–100 properties who have budget and centralized operations teams.
