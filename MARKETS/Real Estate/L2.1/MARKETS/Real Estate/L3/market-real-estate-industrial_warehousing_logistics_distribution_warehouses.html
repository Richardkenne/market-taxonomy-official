Logistics & Distribution Warehouses

Logistics and distribution warehouses are large-format industrial buildings — typically 200,000–1M+ sq ft — designed for bulk storage, sortation, and outbound distribution of goods across regional and national supply chains.

Market data is sourced from CBRE Industrial & Logistics reports, Prologis Research, and JLL Industrial Outlook tracking net absorption, vacancy rates, and rent growth across major distribution markets.

Sources

CBRE Industrial & Logistics Prologis Research JLL Industrial Outlook Cushman & Wakefield Industrial

Market Context

Size / Scale ~19B sq ft of industrial space in the US; logistics/distribution subset ~40–50% of total; Prologis alone owns 1.2B sq ft globally
Growth signal Vacancy rising from historic lows (2%) toward 5–6% as e-commerce growth normalizes; rents elevated but growth moderating; reshoring and nearshoring creating new demand nodes
Key players Prologis (global REIT, 1.2B sq ft), Duke Realty (acquired by Prologis), Blackstone Real Estate (private owner), Panattoni (developer), IDI Logistics (developer)
Geography Inland Empire CA, Dallas-Fort Worth, Chicago O'Hare corridor, Atlanta, Central PA (I-78/81), NJ/NY metro, Memphis, Louisville; port-adjacent and interstate-proximate sites command premium
Distribution warehouse owners and operators manage complex tenant relationships with logistics operators who have sophisticated operational requirements — creating durable opportunities in lease management, building analytics, and tenant services.

Known Pain Points

  • Dock door scheduling and yard management are handled by tenants internally with no landlord visibility — creating friction on shared-facility properties and missed revenue for landlords with multi-tenant buildings
  • Lease administration for NNN industrial leases involves HVAC, roof, and structural responsibility allocations that require precise tracking of landlord vs. tenant obligations — typically done in spreadsheets
  • Building condition assessment and preventive maintenance tracking for roof systems, dock levelers, and HVAC across a portfolio of large-format buildings lacks purpose-built software for landlord teams
  • Energy and utility monitoring for high-cube warehouses (lighting, dock seals, HVAC) is not tracked at the asset level, leaving significant utility cost reduction opportunities uncaptured

How to Choose a Niche

Target industrial REITs and private equity-owned portfolios (20–200 building portfolios) rather than individual building owners. Portfolio-level asset management and CapEx planning tools are the highest-value entry point. Tenant services platforms (dock scheduling, work order submission, building access) for multi-tenant industrial parks are a differentiated wedge that improves NOI and tenant retention.
