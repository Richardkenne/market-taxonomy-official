Foundation

What Are Business Domains & Why 12?

The structural basis of the framework — and why Domains are distinct from Business Systems and Delivery Models.

Executive Statement

Every business operates across twelve functional domains. These are not departments — they are the universal areas in which all value is created, captured, and sustained, regardless of market, industry, or technology.

The 12 Business Domains are orthogonal to the 12 Business Systems and 12 Delivery Models. Systems answer "how does money enter?". Delivery Models answer "how does value reach the user?". Domains answer "what does the business actually do to create and sustain that value?"

Definition

A Business Domain is a functional area of activity that:

  • exists in every business at some level of sophistication,
  • is independent of revenue model or delivery mechanism,
  • represents a distinct set of decisions, resources, and trade-offs,
  • and has measurable impact on competitive position or profitability.

If removing a domain from the business does not affect its ability to create or capture value, it was never a domain — it was overhead.

The 12 Domains

01
Product Development & Innovation

Research, design, engineering, and iteration. The primary source of differentiation. Without it, the business commoditises — competing solely on price or distribution.

02
Pricing & Revenue Optimization

Monetisation model, price level, packaging, and payment terms. Often the highest-leverage decision in the business: a 10% price improvement flows directly to operating profit without additional cost.

03
Operations & Execution

Manufacturing, delivery, and fulfillment. Controls gross margin and quality. Operational inefficiency is permanent — it does not self-correct and compounds over time.

04
Market Access & Distribution

Channels, logistics, and infrastructure to reach customers. A great product in the wrong channel fails. Distribution determines whether unit economics are achievable at scale.

05
Customer Acquisition & Growth

Sales, marketing, and growth strategy. Determines CAC, revenue ramp, and market share. A business without a repeatable acquisition engine is a project, not a business.

06
Customer Retention & Success

Support, success management, and churn reduction. In recurring-revenue models, retention economics typically exceed acquisition economics — yet most businesses under-invest here.

07
Data & Insight

Analytics, metrics, and decision intelligence. Turns raw signals into strategic clarity. In some business models (Bloomberg, Nielsen, Palantir), data itself becomes the sellable product.

08
Network Effects & Community

Two-sided dynamics, platform liquidity, and community engagement. The most durable moat in business because it compounds: value increases with scale, making imitation progressively harder.

09
Brand & Market Position

Positioning, identity, and perception. Strong brands command 10–30% pricing premiums and lower CAC through organic demand. Brand is a long-term asset — it cannot be created quickly.

10
Partnerships & Ecosystem

Strategic alliances, integrations, and platform relationships. Good partnerships multiply reach without proportional cost. Bad partnerships drain resources without reciprocal value.

11
People & Organisation

Hiring, culture, and team capability. Payroll typically represents 50–70% of operating expense. The quality of this domain determines whether any strategy gets executed — or simply documented.

12
Finance & Capital

Funding, cash management, and investor relations. Determines runway, growth capacity, and exit optionality. Running out of cash kills profitable businesses as effectively as unprofitable ones.

Domains vs Systems vs Delivery Models

These three frameworks are orthogonal — they answer different questions:

  • Business Systems — how does money enter the firm? (subscription, transaction, licensing, etc.)
  • Delivery Models — how does value reach the user? (SaaS, physical, on-site, platform, etc.)
  • Business Domains — what does the business do to create and sustain that value?

A SaaS company (Delivery Model: 01) using subscriptions (Business System: 01) still operates across all 12 domains — its competitive position is determined by how well it executes each of them relative to alternatives.

Why the number is 12

The number 12 is not arbitrary. It emerges from convergent analysis of:

  • Porter's Value Chain — primary and support activities cover the same functional territory, resolved to 12 non-overlapping domains.
  • Corporate function taxonomy — every public company organises its operations across the same recurring set of functional areas. None has introduced a 13th that is not a sub-function of an existing one.
  • Strategic frameworks (McKinsey 7-S, OKR structures, balanced scorecards) — each resolves to the same 12 areas when collapsed to first principles.
  • Job-to-be-done analysis — the functional "jobs" a business must perform for customers, investors, and itself map to exactly these 12 categories.

Every proposed 13th domain is either a sub-domain of an existing one or a combination of two.

The rule: A business that ignores any domain does not eliminate it — it outsources it, neglects it, or lets it degrade by default. The question is never whether a domain exists, but who owns it and how well it is being executed.

Mapping your business across all 12 domains reveals not just what you are doing, but what you are not doing — and whether that is a deliberate strategic choice or an invisible gap.
